<p><b>Ancient Greek</b> includes the forms of <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">ancient world</a> from around the 9th century BCE to the 6th century CE. It is often roughly divided into the <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Greece" title="Archaic Greece">Archaic</a> period (9th to 6th centuries BCE), <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">Classical</a> period (5th and 4th centuries BCE), and <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> (3rd century BCE to 6th century CE). It is antedated in the second millennium BCE by <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Mycenaean Greek language">Mycenaean Greek</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> of the Hellenistic phase is known as <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine</a> (common). Koine is regarded as a separate historical stage of its own, although in its earliest form it closely resembled <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">Attic Greek</a> and in its latest form it approaches <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Greek" title="Medieval Greek">Medieval Greek</a>. Prior to the Koine period, Greek of the classic and earlier periods included several <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_dialects" title="Ancient Greek dialects">regional dialects</a>.</p>
<p>Ancient Greek was the language of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Pericles" class="mw-redirect" title="Age of Pericles">classical Athenian</a> historians, playwrights, and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy" title="Ancient Greek philosophy">philosophers</a>. It has contributed many words to English vocabulary and has been a standard subject of study in educational institutions of the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">West</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>. This article primarily contains information about the Epic and Classical phases of the language.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Greek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_dialects" title="Ancient Greek dialects">Ancient Greek dialects</a></div>
<p>Ancient Greek was a <a href="/wiki/Pluricentric_language" title="Pluricentric language">pluricentric language</a>, divided into many dialects. The main dialect groups are <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">Attic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ionic_Greek" title="Ionic Greek">Ionic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aeolic_Greek" title="Aeolic Greek">Aeolic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arcadocypriot_Greek" title="Arcadocypriot Greek">Arcadocypriot</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Doric_Greek" title="Doric Greek">Doric</a>, many of them with several subdivisions. Some dialects are found in standardized literary forms used in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_literature" title="Ancient Greek literature">literature</a>, while others are attested only in inscriptions.</p>
<p>There are also several historical forms. <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Greek" title="Homeric Greek">Homeric Greek</a> is a literary form of Archaic Greek (derived primarily from Ionic and Aeolic) used in the <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poems</a>, the "<a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a>", and in later poems by other authors. Homeric Greek had significant differences in grammar and pronunciation from Classical Attic and other Classical-era dialects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Greek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The origins, early form and development of the Hellenic language family are not well understood because of a lack of contemporaneous evidence. Several theories exist about what Hellenic dialect groups may have existed between the divergence of early Greek-like speech from the common <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European language</a>. They have the same general outline, but differ in some of the detail. The only attested dialect from this period<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek" title="Mycenaean Greek">Mycenaean Greek</a>, but its relationship to the historical dialects and the historical circumstances of the times imply that the overall groups already existed in some form.</p>
<p>Scholars assume that major Ancient Greek period dialect groups developed not later than 1120 BC, at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Dorian_invasion" title="Dorian invasion">Dorian invasion</a>(s)â€”and that their first appearances as precise alphabetic writing began in the 8th century BC. The invasion would not be "Dorian" unless the invaders had some cultural relationship to the historical <a href="/wiki/Dorians" title="Dorians">Dorians</a>. The invasion is known to have displaced population to the later Attic-Ionic regions, who regarded themselves as descendants of the population displaced by or contending with the Dorians.</p>
<p>The Greeks of this period believed there were three major divisions of all Greek peopleâ€”Dorians, Aeolians, and Ionians (including Athenians), each with their own defining and distinctive dialects. Allowing for their oversight of Arcadian, an obscure mountain dialect, and Cypriot, far from the center of Greek scholarship, this division of people and language is quite similar to the results of modern archaeological-linguistic investigation.</p>

<p>West vs. non-west Greek is the strongest marked and earliest division, with non-west in subsets of Ionic-Attic (or Attic-Ionic) and Aeolic vs. Arcadocypriot, or Aeolic and Arcado-Cypriot vs. Ionic-Attic. Often non-west is called East Greek.</p>
<p>Arcadocypriot apparently descended more closely from the Mycenaean Greek of the Bronze Age.</p>
<p>Boeotian had come under a strong Northwest Greek influence, and can in some respects be considered a transitional dialect. Thessalian likewise had come under Northwest Greek influence, though to a lesser degree.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pamphylian_Greek" title="Pamphylian Greek">Pamphylian Greek</a>, spoken in a small area on the southwestern coast of Anatolia and little preserved in inscriptions, may be either a fifth major dialect group, or it is Mycenaean Greek overlaid by Doric, with a non-Greek native influence.</p>
<p>Most of the dialect sub-groups listed above had further subdivisions, generally equivalent to a city-state and its surrounding territory, or to an island. Doric notably had several intermediate divisions as well, into Island Doric (including <a href="/wiki/Doric_Greek#Cretan" title="Doric Greek">Cretan Doric</a>), Southern Peloponnesus Doric (including <a href="/wiki/Doric_Greek#Laconian.2C_Heraclean" title="Doric Greek">Laconian</a>, the dialect of <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a>), and Northern Peloponnesus Doric (including <a href="/wiki/Doric_Greek#Corinthian" title="Doric Greek">Corinthian</a>).</p>
<p>The Lesbian dialect was <a href="/wiki/Aeolic_Greek" title="Aeolic Greek">Aeolic Greek</a>.</p>
<p>All the groups were represented by colonies beyond Greece proper as well, and these colonies generally developed local characteristics, often under the influence of settlers or neighbors speaking different Greek dialects.</p>
<p>The dialects outside the Ionic group are known mainly from inscriptions, notable exceptions being fragments of the works of the poet <a href="/wiki/Sappho" title="Sappho">Sappho</a> from the island of <a href="/wiki/Lesbos" title="Lesbos">Lesbos</a> and the poems of the <a href="/wiki/Boeotia" title="Boeotia">Boeotian</a> poet, <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a>.</p>
<p>After the conquests of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> in the late 4th century BCE, a new international dialect known as <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine</a> or Common Greek developed, largely based on <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">Attic Greek</a>, but with influence from other dialects. This dialect slowly replaced most of the older dialects, although Doric dialect has survived in the <a href="/wiki/Tsakonian_language" title="Tsakonian language">Tsakonian language</a>, which is spoken in the region of modern Sparta. Doric has also passed down its aorist terminations into most verbs of <a href="/wiki/Demotic_Greek" title="Demotic Greek">Demotic Greek</a>. By about the 6th century CE, the Koine had slowly metamorphosized into <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Greek" title="Medieval Greek">Medieval Greek</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">Other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Greek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Macedonian_language" title="Ancient Macedonian language">Ancient Macedonian</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> language closely related to Greek, but its exact relationship is unclear because of insufficient data: possibly a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_dialects" title="Ancient Greek dialects">dialect of Greek</a>; a sibling language to Greek; or a close cousin to Greek, and perhaps related to some extent, to <a href="/wiki/Thracian_language" title="Thracian language">Thracian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phrygian_language" title="Phrygian language">Phrygian</a> languages. The <a href="/wiki/Pella_curse_tablet" title="Pella curse tablet">Pella curse tablet</a> is one of many finds that support the idea that the Ancient Macedonian language is closely related to the Doric Greek dialect. However, Ancient Macedonian was definitely not a Slavic language; Slavs migrated in Eastern Europe nine centuries later.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonemic_inventory">Phonemic inventory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Greek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonemic inventory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_phonology" title="Ancient Greek phonology">Ancient Greek phonology</a></div>
<p>The pronunciation of Ancient Greek was very different from that of Modern Greek. Ancient Greek had <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">long and short vowels</a>; many <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthongs</a>; <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">double</a> and single consonants; voiced, voiceless, and aspirated <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stops</a>; and a <a href="/wiki/Pitch_accent" title="Pitch accent">pitch accent</a>. In Modern Greek, all vowels and consonants are shortâ€”many vowels and diphthongs once pronounced distinctly are pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span> (<a href="/wiki/Iotacism" title="Iotacism">iotacism</a>). Some of the stops and <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">glides</a> in diphthongs have become <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">fricatives</a>, and the pitch accent has changed to a <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">stress accent</a>. Many of these changes took place in the <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a> period. The writing system of Modern Greek, however, does not reflect all pronunciation changes.</p>
<p>The examples below represent Attic Greek in the 5th century BCE. Ancient pronunciation can never be reconstructed with certainty, but Greek from this period is well documented and there is little disagreement among linguists as to the general nature of the sounds that the letters represent.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Greek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_grammar" title="Ancient Greek grammar">Ancient Greek grammar</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AGMA_Ostrakon_Cimon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/AGMA_Ostrakon_Cimon.jpg/220px-AGMA_Ostrakon_Cimon.jpg" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/AGMA_Ostrakon_Cimon.jpg/330px-AGMA_Ostrakon_Cimon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/AGMA_Ostrakon_Cimon.jpg/440px-AGMA_Ostrakon_Cimon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="506" /></a>

<a href="/wiki/Ostracon" title="Ostracon">Ostracon</a> bearing the name of <a href="/wiki/Cimon" title="Cimon">Cimon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoa_of_Attalos" title="Stoa of Attalos">Stoa of Attalos</a></div>

<p>Greek, like all of the older <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a>, is highly inflected. It is highly archaic in its preservation of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> forms. In Ancient Greek, <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a> (including proper nouns) have five <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">cases</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">nominative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">accusative</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vocative_case" title="Vocative case">vocative</a>), three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">genders</a> (<a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">masculine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">feminine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">neuter</a>), and three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">numbers</a> (singular, <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">plural</a>). <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">Verbs</a> have four <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">moods</a> (<a href="/wiki/Realis_mood" title="Realis mood">indicative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">imperative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subjunctive_mood" title="Subjunctive mood">subjunctive</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Optative_mood" title="Optative mood">optative</a>) and three <a href="/wiki/Voice_(grammar)" title="Voice (grammar)">voices</a> (active, middle, and <a href="/wiki/English_passive_voice" title="English passive voice">passive</a>), as well as three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">persons</a> (first, second, and third) and various other forms. Verbs are conjugated through seven combinations of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">tenses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">aspect</a> (generally simply called "tenses"): the <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">present</a>, <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">future</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">imperfect</a> are <a href="/wiki/Imperfective_aspect" title="Imperfective aspect">imperfective</a> in aspect; the <a href="/wiki/Aorist" title="Aorist">aorist</a> (<a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect">perfective aspect</a>); a <a href="/wiki/Present_perfect" title="Present perfect">present perfect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pluperfect" title="Pluperfect">pluperfect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Future_perfect" title="Future perfect">future perfect</a>. Most tenses display all four moods and three voices, although there is no future subjunctive or imperative. Also, there is no imperfect subjunctive, optative or imperative. The infinitives and participles correspond to the finite combinations of tense, aspect, and voice.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Augment">Augment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Greek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Augment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The indicative of past <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">tenses</a> adds (conceptually, at least) a prefix /e-/, called the <a href="/wiki/Augment_(linguistics)" title="Augment (linguistics)">augment</a>. This was probably originally a separate word, meaning something like "then", added because tenses in PIE had primarily aspectual meaning. The augment is added to the <a href="/wiki/Realis_mood" title="Realis mood">indicative</a> of the aorist, imperfect, and pluperfect, but not to any of the other forms of the aorist (no other forms of the imperfect and pluperfect exist).</p>
<p>The two kinds of augment in Greek are syllabic and quantitative. The syllabic augment is added to stems beginning with consonants, and simply prefixes <i>e</i> (stems beginning with <i>r</i>, however, add <i>er</i>). The quantitative augment is added to stems beginning with vowels, and involves lengthening the vowel:</p>
<ul>
<li>a, Ä?, e, Ä“ â†’ Ä“</li>
<li>i, Ä« â†’ Ä«</li>
<li>o, Å? â†’ Å?</li>
<li>u, Å« â†’ Å«</li>
<li>ai â†’ Ä“i</li>
<li>ei â†’ Ä“i or ei</li>
<li>oi â†’ Å?i</li>
<li>au â†’ Ä“u or au</li>
<li>eu â†’ Ä“u or eu</li>
<li>ou â†’ ou</li>
</ul>
<p>Some verbs augment irregularly; the most common variation is <i>e</i> â†’ <i>ei</i>. The irregularity can be explained diachronically by the loss of <i>s</i> between vowels. In verbs with a prefix, the augment is placed not at the start of the word, but between the prefix and the original verb. For example, Ï€Ï?Î¿Ïƒ(-)Î²Î¬Î»Î»Ï‰ (I attack) goes to Ï€Ï?Î¿Ïƒ<b>Î­</b>Î²Î±Î»oÎ½ in the aorist.</p>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s practice, the augment is sometimes not made in <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Greek" title="Homeric Greek">epic</a> poetry.</p>
<p>The augment sometimes substitutes for reduplication; see below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduplication">Reduplication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Greek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reduplication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Almost all forms of the perfect, pluperfect, and future perfect reduplicate the initial syllable of the verb stem. (Note that a few irregular forms of perfect do not reduplicate, whereas a handful of irregular aorists reduplicate.) The three types of <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">reduplication</a> are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Syllabic reduplication: Most verbs beginning with a single consonant, or a cluster of a stop with a sonorant, add a syllable consisting of the initial consonant followed by <i>e</i>. An aspirated consonant, however, reduplicates in its unaspirated equivalent: <a href="/wiki/Grassmann%27s_law" title="Grassmann's law">Grassmann's law</a>.</li>
<li>Augment: Verbs beginning with a vowel, as well as those beginning with a cluster other than those indicated previously (and occasionally for a few other verbs) reduplicate in the same fashion as the augment. This remains in all forms of the perfect, not just the indicative.</li>
<li>Attic reduplication: Some verbs beginning with an <i>a</i>, <i>e</i> or <i>o</i>, followed by a sonorant (or occasionally <i>d</i> or <i>g</i>), reduplicate by adding a syllable consisting of the initial vowel and following consonant, and lengthening the following vowel. Hence <i>er</i> â†’ <i>erÄ“r</i>, <i>an</i> â†’ <i>anÄ“n</i>, <i>ol</i> â†’ <i>olÅ?l</i>, <i>ed</i> â†’ <i>edÄ“d</i>. This is not actually specific to <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">Attic Greek</a>, despite its name, but it was generalized in Attic. This originally involved reduplicating a cluster consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Laryngeal_theory" title="Laryngeal theory">laryngeal</a> and sonorant, hence <i>hâ‚ƒl</i> â†’ <i>hâ‚ƒlehâ‚ƒl</i> â†’ <i>olÅ?l</i> with normal Greek development of laryngeals. (Forms with a stop were analogous.)</li>
</ul>
<p>Irregular duplication can be understood diachronically. For example, <i>lambanÅ?</i> (root <i>lab</i>) has the perfect stem <i>eilÄ“pha</i> (not *<i>lelÄ“pha</i>) because it was originally <i>slambanÅ?</i>, with perfect <i>seslÄ“pha</i>, becoming <i>eilÄ“pha</i> through compensatory lengthening.</p>
<p>Reduplication is also visible in the present tense stems of certain verbs. These stems add a syllable consisting of the root's initial consonant followed by <i>i</i>. A nasal stop appears after the reduplication in some verbs.<sup id="cite_ref-Palmer1996p262_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer1996p262-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greek_orthography" title="Greek orthography">Greek orthography</a></div>
<p>Ancient Greek was written in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a>, with some variation among dialects. Early texts are written in <a href="/wiki/Boustrophedon" title="Boustrophedon">boustrophedon</a> style, but left-to-right became standard during the classic period. Modern editions of Ancient Greek texts are usually written with <a href="/wiki/Greek_diacritics" title="Greek diacritics">accents and breathing marks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Word_divider" title="Word divider">interword spacing</a>, modern <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">punctuation</a>, and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Capitalization" title="Capitalization">mixed case</a>, but these were all introduced later.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_use">Modern use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Greek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modern use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Classical_compound" title="Classical compound">Classical compound</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Molon_labe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Molon_labe.jpg/220px-Molon_labe.jpg" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Molon_labe.jpg/330px-Molon_labe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Molon_labe.jpg/440px-Molon_labe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2390" data-file-height="1662" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Molon_labe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The phrase "<a href="/wiki/Molon_labe" title="Molon labe">molon labe</a>" as inscribed on the marble of the 1955 <a href="/wiki/Leonidas_Monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Leonidas Monument">Leonidas Monument</a> at <a href="/wiki/Thermopylae" title="Thermopylae">Thermopylae</a></div>

<p>The study of Ancient Greek in European countries in addition to <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> occupied an important place in the syllabus from the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> until the beginning of the 20th century. Ancient Greek is still taught as a compulsory or optional subject especially at traditional or elite schools throughout Europe, such as <a href="/wiki/Independent_school_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent school (UK)">public schools</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar schools</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It is compulsory in the <a href="/wiki/Liceo_classico" title="Liceo classico">Liceo classico</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, in the gymnasium in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, in some classes in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> in <i>klasiÄ?na gimnazija</i>, in Classical Studies in <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Belgium#Structure" title="Education in Belgium">ASO</a> in Belgium and it is optional in the <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Humanistisches Gymnasium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> (usually as a third language after Latin and English, from the age of 14 to 18). In 2006/07, 15,000 pupils studied Ancient Greek in Germany according to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Statistical_Office_of_Germany" title="Federal Statistical Office of Germany">Federal Statistical Office of Germany</a>, and 280,000 pupils studied it in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is a compulsory subject alongside Latin in the Humanities branch of <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Spain#Spanish_Baccalaureate_.28Bachillerato.29" title="Education in Spain">Spanish Bachillerato</a>. Ancient Greek is also taught at most major <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> worldwide, often combined with <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">Classics</a>. It will also be taught in state primary schools in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, to boost childrenâ€™s language skills,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and will be offered as a foreign language to pupils in all primary schools from 2014 as part of a major drive to boost education standards, together with Latin, Mandarin, French, German, Spanish, and Italian.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Ancient Greek is also taught as a compulsory subject in <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Gymnasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyceum" title="Lyceum">Lykia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern authors rarely write in Ancient Greek, though <a href="/wiki/Jan_K%C5%99esadlo" title="Jan KÅ™esadlo">Jan KÅ™esadlo</a> wrote some poetry and prose in the language, and some volumes of <a href="/wiki/Asterix" title="Asterix">Asterix</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone" title="Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone">Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> have been translated into Ancient Greek. á½ˆÎ½ÏŒÎ¼Î±Ï„Î± KÎµÏ‡Î¹Î±ÏƒÎ¼Î­Î½Î± (<i><a href="/wiki/Onomata_Kechiasmena" title="Onomata Kechiasmena">Onomata Kechiasmena</a></i>) is the first magazine of crosswords and puzzles in Ancient Greek.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Its first issue appeared in April 2015 as an annex to <i><a href="/wiki/Hebdomada_Aenigmatum" title="Hebdomada Aenigmatum">Hebdomada Aenigmatum</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Rahlfs" title="Alfred Rahlfs">Alfred Rahlfs</a> included a preface, a short history of the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a> text, and other <a href="/wiki/Front_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Front matter">front matter</a> translated into Ancient Greek in his 1935 edition of the Septuagint; Robert Hanhart also included the introductory remarks to the 2006 revised Rahlfsâ€“Hanhart edition in the language as well.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ancient Greek is also used by organizations and individuals, mainly Greek, who wish to denote their respect, admiration or preference for the use of this language. This use is sometimes considered graphical, nationalistic or funny. In any case, the fact that modern Greeks can still wholly or partly understand texts written in non-archaic forms of ancient Greek shows the affinity of modern Greek language to its ancestral predecessor.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>An isolated community near <a href="/wiki/Trabzon" title="Trabzon">Trabzon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, an area where <a href="/wiki/Pontic_Greek" title="Pontic Greek">Pontic Greek</a> is spoken, has been found to speak a variety of Greek that has parallels, both structurally and in its vocabulary, to Ancient Greek not present in other varieties.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> As few as 5,000 people speak the dialect but linguists believe that it is the closest living language to Ancient Greek.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ancient Greek is often used in the coinage of modern technical terms in the European languages: see <a href="/wiki/English_words_of_Greek_origin" title="English words of Greek origin">English words of Greek origin</a>. <a href="/wiki/Latinisation_of_names" title="Latinisation of names">Latinized</a> forms of Ancient Greek roots are used in many of the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific names</a> of <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and in scientific terminology.</p>
