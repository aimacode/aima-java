<p>A <b>philosopher</b> is someone who practices <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, which involves <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">rational</a> inquiry into areas that are outside of either <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theological</a> <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">dogma</a> or <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RussellHistory10_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RussellHistory10-1">[1]</a></sup> The term "philosopher" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ï†Î¹Î»ÏŒÏƒÎ¿Ï†Î¿Ï‚</span> (<i>philosophos</i>) meaning "lover of wisdom". Its origination has been ascribed to the Greek thinker <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tufts_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tufts-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">classical</a> sense, a philosopher was someone who lived according to a certain way of life, focusing on resolving <a href="/wiki/Meaning_of_life" title="Meaning of life">existential questions</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">human condition</a>, and not someone who discourses upon theories or comments upon authors.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Typically, these particular brands of philosophy are <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy" title="Hellenistic philosophy">Hellenistic</a> ones and those who most arduously commit themselves to this lifestyle may be considered philosophers.</p>
<p>In a modern sense, a philosopher is an <a href="/wiki/Intellectual" title="Intellectual">intellectual</a> who has contributed in one or more branches of philosophy, such as <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_theory" title="Social theory">social theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>. A philosopher may also be one who worked in the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> or other sciences which have since split from philosophy proper over the centuries, such as <a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">the arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philosopher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">This section is about the history of philosophers as an occupation. For a history of philosophy in general, see <a href="/wiki/History_of_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="History of philosophy">History of philosophy</a>.</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Greece_and_Rome">Ancient Greece and Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philosopher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient Greece and Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The separation of philosophy and science from theology began in Greece during the <a href="/wiki/6th_century_BC" title="6th century BC">6th century BC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RussellHistory11_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RussellHistory11-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thales" title="Thales">Thales</a>, an astronomer and mathematician, was considered by Aristotle to be the first philosopher of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy" title="Ancient Greek philosophy">Greek tradition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a> coined the word, the first known elaboration on the topic was conducted by <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>. In his <i><a href="/wiki/Symposium_(Plato)" title="Symposium (Plato)">Symposium</a></i>, he concludes that Love is that which lacks the object it seeks. Therefore, the philosopher is one who seeks wisdom; if he attains wisdom, he would be a <a href="/wiki/Sage_(philosophy)" title="Sage (philosophy)">sage</a>. Therefore, the philosopher in antiquity was one who lives in the constant pursuit of wisdom, and living in accordance to that wisdom.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Disagreements arose as to what living philosophically entailed. These disagreements gave rise to different <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy" title="Hellenistic philosophy">Hellenistic schools of philosophy</a>. In consequence, the ancient philosopher thought in a tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> As the ancient world became schism by philosophical debate, the competition lay in living in manner that would transform his whole way of living in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the last of these philosophers was <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>, who is widely regarded as a philosopher in the modern sense, but personally refused to call himself by such a title, since he had a <a href="/wiki/Duty" title="Duty">duty</a> to live as an <a href="/wiki/List_of_Roman_emperors" title="List of Roman emperors">emperor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition">Transition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philosopher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Classicist" class="mw-redirect" title="Classicist">Classicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Hadot" title="Pierre Hadot">Pierre Hadot</a>, the modern conception of a philosopher and philosophy developed predominately through three changes:</p>
<p>The first is the natural inclination of the philosophical mind. Philosophy is a tempting discipline which can easily carry away the individual in analyzing the universe and abstract theory.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second is the historical change through the Medieval era. With the rise of Christianity, the philosophical way of life was adopted by its theology. Thus, philosophy was divided between a way of life and the conceptual, logical, physical and metaphysical materials to justify that way of life. Philosophy was then the servant to theology.<sup id="cite_ref-Hadot32_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hadot32-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The third is the sociological need with the development of the university. The modern university requires professionals to teach. Maintaining itself requires teaching future professionals to replace the current faculty. Therefore, the discipline degrades into a technical language reserved for specialists, completely eschewing its original conception as a way of life.<sup id="cite_ref-Hadot32_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hadot32-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_era">Medieval era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philosopher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medieval era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the fourth century, the word philosopher began to designate a man or woman who led a monastic life. <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Nyssa" title="Gregory of Nyssa">Gregory of Nyssa</a>, for example, describes how his sister <a href="/wiki/Macrina_the_Younger" title="Macrina the Younger">Macrina</a> persuaded <a href="/wiki/Emmelia_of_Caesarea" title="Emmelia of Caesarea">their mother</a> to forsake "the distractions of material life" for a life of philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, persons who engaged with <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a> was called a philosopher - thus, the <a href="/wiki/Philosopher%27s_Stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Philosopher's Stone">Philosopher's Stone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_era">Early Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philosopher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="rquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">
<tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">â€œ</td>
<td style="padding:0 10px;"><i>Generally speaking, university philosophy is mere fencing in front of a mirror. In the last analysis, its goal is to give students opinions which are to the liking of the minister who hands out the Chairs... As a result, this state-financed philosophy makes a joke of philosophy. And yet, if there is one thing desirable in this world, it is to see a ray of light fall onto the darkness of our lives, shedding some kind of light on the mysterious enigma of our existence.</i></td>

<td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px; font-size:smaller; line-height:1em; text-align:right;"><cite>â€”&#160;<a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Arthur Schopenhauer</a></cite></td>

<p>Many philosophers still emerged from the Classical tradition, as saw their philosophy as a way of life. Among the most notable are <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="RenÃ© Descartes">RenÃ© Descartes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza" title="Baruch Spinoza">Baruch Spinoza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Malebranche" title="Nicolas Malebranche">Nicolas Malebranche</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a>. With the rise of the university, the modern conception of philosophy became more prominent. Many of the esteemed philosophers of the eighteenth century and onward have attended, taught, and developed their works in university. Early examples include: <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottlieb_Fichte" title="Johann Gottlieb Fichte">Johann Gottlieb Fichte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Joseph_Schelling" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling">Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>After these individuals, the Classical conception had all but died with the exceptions of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Arthur Schopenhauer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a>. The last considerable figure in philosophy to not have followed a strict and orthodox academic regime was <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Ludwig Wittgenstein</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_academia">Modern academia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philosopher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern academia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the modern era, those attaining advanced degrees in philosophy often choose to stay in careers within the educational system. According to a 1993 study by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a> (as reported by the <a href="/wiki/American_Philosophical_Association" title="American Philosophical Association">American Philosophical Association</a>), 77.1% of the 7,900 holders of a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> in philosophy who responded were employed in educational institutions (<a href="/wiki/Academia" title="Academia">academia</a>). Outside of academia, philosophers may employ their writing and reasoning skills in other careers, such as <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (November 2015)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Bioethics" title="Bioethics">bioethics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a>, <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">publishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Free-lance_writing" class="mw-redirect" title="Free-lance writing">free-lance writing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Media_(communication)" title="Media (communication)">media</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-APA-nonacademic_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APA-nonacademic-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prizes_in_philosophy">Prizes in philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philosopher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prizes in philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
