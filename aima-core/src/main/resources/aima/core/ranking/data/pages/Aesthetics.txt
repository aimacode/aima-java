<p><b>Aesthetics</b>, or the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> of art, is the study of beauty and taste. It is about interpreting works of art and art movements or theories. The term aesthetic is also used to designate a particular style, for example the "<a href="/wiki/Chess_aesthetics" title="Chess aesthetics">chess aesthetics</a>", the "<a href="/wiki/Japanese_aesthetics" title="Japanese aesthetics">japanese aesthetics</a>".</p>
<p>As well as being applied to art, aesthetics can also be applied to cultural objects. Aesthetic design principles include ornamentation, edge delineation, texture, flow, solemnity, symmetry, color, granularity, the interaction of sunlight and shadows, transcendence, and harmony.</p>
<p>The word aesthetic is also an adjective and adverb relating to <a href="/wiki/Cosmetology" title="Cosmetology">cosmetology</a> and medicine, as in <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic_medicine" title="Aesthetic medicine">aesthetic medicine</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_aesthetics_in_western_philosophy">History of aesthetics in western philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of aesthetics in western philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aesthetica.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Aesthetica.png/220px-Aesthetica.png" width="220" height="352" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Aesthetica.png/330px-Aesthetica.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9a/Aesthetica.png 2x" data-file-width="383" data-file-height="613" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aesthetica.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gottlieb_Baumgarten" title="Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten">Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten</a>'s Aesthetica</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_aesthetics_before_the_20th_century" title="History of aesthetics before the 20th century">History of aesthetics before the 20th century</a></div>
<p>The idea of the aesthetic developed from the idea of <a href="/wiki/Taste_(sociology)" title="Taste (sociology)">taste</a> and beauty. Before the early 1700s, thinkers developed general theories of proportion and harmony, detailed most specifically in architecture and music. An extended, philosophical reflection on aesthetics emerged with the widening of leisure activities in the eighteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1700s, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a> tried to explain aesthetic concepts such as beauty with <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">empirical evidence</a>, by connecting them with typical individuals' responses. They sought a basis for an objectivity of personal reactions.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1800s psychologist <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Wundt" title="Wilhelm Wundt">Wilhelm Wundt</a> showed that interest is generally related to complexity of stimulus. To arouse interest an object should be neither boringly simple nor overly complex; thus complexity could be an objective measure. It is now known, for instance, that judgments of facial beauty in humans are a matter of averageness and symmetry.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The analysis of individual experience and behavior based on <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiment</a> is a central part of <a href="/wiki/Experimental_aesthetics" title="Experimental aesthetics">experimental aesthetics</a>, a field founded by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Theodor_Fechner" class="mw-redirect" title="Gustav Theodor Fechner">Gustav Theodor Fechner</a> in the 1800s.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a> insisted that aesthetic concepts are essentially subjective, but have some objectivity since feelings of pleasure and pain can be universal responses to certain stimuli.</p>
<p>Recently theorists have been interested in ways that aesthetic concepts are constructed out of social mores and practices. Evaluations of beauty may well be linked to desirability, economic, political, or moral value. One might judge a <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini" title="Lamborghini">Lamborghini</a> to be beautiful partly because it is desirable as a status symbol, or we might judge it to be repulsive partly because it signifies for us over-consumption and offends our political or moral values.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As late as 1912 it was normal in the West to assume that all art aims at beauty, and thus that anything that wasn't trying to be beautiful couldn't count as art. The <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">cubists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">dadaists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Stravinsky</a>, and many later <a href="/wiki/Art_movement" title="Art movement">art movements</a> struggled against this conception that beauty was central to the definition of art, with such success that, according to <a href="/wiki/Danto" class="mw-redirect" title="Danto">Danto</a>, "Beauty had disappeared not only from the advanced art of the 1960s but from the advanced philosophy of art of that decade as well."</p>
<p>In the 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">Walter Benjamin</a>, in his essay <a href="/wiki/The_Work_of_Art_in_the_Age_of_Mechanical_Reproduction" title="The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction">The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction</a>, argued that, in the absence of any traditional, ritualistic value, art in the age of mechanical reproduction would inherently be based on the practice of politics. <a href="/wiki/John_Berger" title="John Berger">John Berger</a> continued in this direction with <a href="/wiki/Ways_of_Seeing" title="Ways of Seeing">Ways of Seeing</a>, in which he criticizes traditional Western cultural aesthetics by raising questions about hidden ideologies in visual images.</p>
<p>In 1946, <a href="/wiki/W._K._Wimsatt" class="mw-redirect" title="W. K. Wimsatt">William K. Wimsatt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monroe_Beardsley" title="Monroe Beardsley">Monroe Beardsley</a> published the essay <a href="/wiki/Intentional_Fallacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Intentional Fallacy">The Intentional Fallacy</a>, in which they argued strongly against the relevance of an <a href="/wiki/Authorial_intentionality" class="mw-redirect" title="Authorial intentionality">author's intention</a>, or "intended meaning" in the analysis of a literary work. For Wimsatt and Beardsley, the words on the page were all that mattered; importation of meanings from outside the text was considered irrelevant, and potentially distracting. In another essay, "<a href="/wiki/Affective_fallacy" title="Affective fallacy">The Affective Fallacy</a>," which served as a kind of sister essay to "The Intentional Fallacy", Wimsatt and Beardsley also discounted the reader's personal/emotional reaction to a literary work as a valid means of analyzing a text. This fallacy would later be repudiated by theorists from the <a href="/wiki/Reader-response" class="mw-redirect" title="Reader-response">reader-response</a> school of literary theory. Ironically, one of the leading theorists from this school, <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Fish" title="Stanley Fish">Stanley Fish</a>, was himself trained by New Critics. Fish criticizes Wimsatt and Beardsley in his essay "Literature in the Reader" (1970).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1959 <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sibley_(philosopher)" title="Frank Sibley (philosopher)">Frank Sibley</a> wrote that aesthetic concepts were not rule- or condition-governed, but required a heightened form of perception, which one might call taste, sensitivity, or judgment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_analysis_of_aesthetics">Scientific analysis of aesthetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scientific analysis of aesthetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg/220px-Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg/330px-Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg/440px-Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>

<p>In the 1990s, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Schmidhuber" title="JÃ¼rgen Schmidhuber">JÃ¼rgen Schmidhuber</a> described an algorithmic theory of beauty which takes the <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjectivity</a> of the observer into account and postulates: among several observations classified as comparable by a given subjective observer, the aesthetically most pleasing one is the one with the shortest description, given the observer's previous knowledge and his particular method for encoding the data. This is closely related to the principles of <a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_information_theory" title="Algorithmic information theory">algorithmic information theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minimum_description_length" title="Minimum description length">minimum description length</a>. For example: <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_beauty" title="Mathematical beauty">mathematical beauty</a>. Another example describes an aesthetically pleasing human face whose proportions can be described with very little information, drawing inspiration from less detailed 15th century proportion studies by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht DÃ¼rer">Albrecht DÃ¼rer</a>. Schmidhuber's theory explicitly distinguishes between what is beauty and what is interesting, stating that the latter corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/First_derivative" class="mw-redirect" title="First derivative">first derivative</a> of subjectively perceived beauty. The premise is that any observer continually tries to improve the predictability and compressibility of the observations by discovering regularities such as repetitions and <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-similarity" title="Self-similarity">self-similarity</a>.</p>
<p>Mathematical considerations, such as symmetry and <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complexity</a>, are used for analysis in theoretical aesthetics. The fact that judgments of beauty and judgments of truth both are influenced by <a href="/wiki/Processing_fluency" title="Processing fluency">processing fluency</a> has been presented as an explanation for why beauty is sometimes equated with truth.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Recent research found that people use beauty as an indication for truth in mathematical pattern tasks.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Computer scientists have attempted to develop automated methods to infer aesthetic quality of images. Typically, these approaches follow a <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a> approach, where large numbers of manually rated photographs are used to teach a computer about what visual properties are of relevance to aesthetic quality. The Acquine engine, developed at <a href="/wiki/Penn_State_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Penn State University">Penn State University</a>, rates natural photographs uploaded by users.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> There have also been relatively successful attempts with regard to chess and music.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_aesthetics" title="Evolutionary aesthetics">Evolutionary aesthetics</a>, the basic aesthetic preferences of humans are argued to be a product of evolutionary adaptations.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> For example, body symmetry may be valued in <a href="/wiki/Physical_attractiveness" title="Physical attractiveness">physical attractiveness</a> because it may indicate good health.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_aesthetics">Japanese aesthetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Japanese aesthetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The study of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_aesthetics" title="Japanese aesthetics">Japanese aesthetics</a> only started a little over two hundred years ago in the West. The Japanese aesthetic is a set of ancient ideals which underpin much of Japanese cultural and aesthetic norms for what is considered tasteful or beautiful. While seen as a philosophy in Western societies, the concept of aesthetics in Japan is seen as an integral part of daily life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_aesthetics">Indian aesthetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Indian aesthetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Indian_aesthetics" title="Indian aesthetics">Indian aesthetics</a> evolved with an emphasis on inducing special spiritual or philosophical states in the audience, or with representing them as <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chess_aesthetics">Chess aesthetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chess aesthetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chess_aesthetics" title="Chess aesthetics">Chess aesthetics</a>, or beauty in chess is appreciated by both players and <a href="/wiki/Chess_composer" title="Chess composer">chess composers</a>. In some tournaments there are prizes for brilliancy (not just for winning a match). There are books published featuring chess problems or puzzles that emphasize their aesthetic aspect. Factors about a game or move sequence (also referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Combination_(chess)" title="Combination (chess)">combination</a>) that might cause it to be regarded as 'brilliant' include: expediency, disguise, sacrifice, correctness, preparation, paradox, unity, and originality.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_aesthetics">Music aesthetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music aesthetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the pre-modern tradition, the <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics_of_music" title="Aesthetics of music">aesthetics of music</a> explored the mathematical and cosmological dimensions of rhythmic and harmonic organization. In the eighteenth century, focus shifted to the experience of hearing music, and thus to questions about its beauty and human enjoyment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_beauty">Mathematical beauty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mathematical beauty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mathematicians consider <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_beauty" title="Mathematical beauty">mathematical beauty</a> to be a desirable quality in their work. Comparisons are often made with music and poetry.</p>
